# Common Interview Questions — Answer Frameworks

## Opening questions (always asked)

### "Tell me about yourself"
**Framework:** Past → Present → Future, in 90 seconds.

> "I've spent the last five years in residential interior design across three firms.
> Most recently I was a Design Consultant at Ethan Allen for three years, where I
> generated over $800,000 in design sales and earned the 2025 Gold Spirit Award.
> Before that I supported a principal designer at Goff Designs and coordinated
> front-of-house operations at Vintage Design Inc. I have a BBA from Arizona State.
>
> What I love about design is the intersection of creative vision and operational
> discipline — making a room feel effortless requires hundreds of small decisions
> handled right.
>
> I'm looking for my next role in [design consultant / showroom / executive assistant
> — match this to the role]. This opportunity caught my eye because [one specific
> thing about the company]."

### "Why are you leaving / why are you looking?"
**Framework:** Never badmouth the employer. Frame as seeking growth.

> "I had a great three years at Ethan Allen and I'm genuinely proud of what I built
> there. I'm looking now because I want to [grow into X / work with Y clientele /
> be closer to Z type of work], and that's harder to do in my current setup. I'm
> being thoughtful about where to land next."

### "Why this company?"
**Framework:** Reference something specific. Show homework.

> "Three reasons. First, [specific thing about their product/market/approach —
> 'Waterworks' focus on luxury fixtures matches exactly the clientele I worked with']
> Second, [cultural thing — 'your reputation for treating clients like partners, not
> transactions']. Third, [personal connection — 'I've admired [specific project,
> designer, award] for a while']. I think there's a real fit here."

## Design-role-specific questions

### "Walk me through your design process"
**Framework:** Listen → Envision → Specify → Deliver

> "It always starts with listening. I spend the first thirty minutes of any
> consultation asking about how the client actually uses the space — who lives
> there, how they entertain, what frustrates them about the current setup. Then I
> translate that into a design direction: layout, palette, materials, lighting.
> From there I build out specific selections — furniture, fabrics, finishes. And I
> stay in the loop through installation to make sure what we designed is what
> actually shows up."

### "How do you handle clients with bad taste?"
**Framework:** This is a trap question — never agree. Reframe.

> "I don't really think of it that way. Clients have preferences, and my job is to
> understand what they're trying to achieve, not judge what they already have. If a
> client wants something that I think won't serve them long-term, I show them why
> with specifics — a fabric that won't wear well with kids, a scale that won't work
> in the room — and then give them alternatives that meet their underlying goal.
> They almost always land on something they're happier with."

### "How do you handle a client who's not happy with your work?"
**Framework:** Listen → Own → Fix

> "First I make sure I understand exactly what's wrong. Usually it's specific — one
> piece, one choice, one expectation we didn't align on. Then I take responsibility
> — even if it's ambiguous whose fault it was. Then I fix it, either by finding an
> alternative selection, offering a credit toward a correction, or in rare cases
> arranging a return. I'd rather lose a dollar than lose the relationship."

## Sales-role-specific questions

### "What's your biggest sale?"
> "My strongest single project was a full home refresh at Ethan Allen — kitchen,
> living room, dining room, and primary bedroom — that came in at about $75,000.
> What I'm most proud of though isn't the single sale, it's the $800K+ I generated
> over three years built on clients who came back for second and third rooms and
> referred friends."

### "Walk me through how you close"
> "I don't really try to 'close' in the traditional sense. By the time I'm presenting
> a proposal, the client and I have already had three or four conversations about
> what they want and what I'm going to recommend. My job in the close is to confirm
> the details — timeline, budget, delivery. The decision has usually been made by
> the time we sit down with paperwork. If it hasn't, something earlier in the
> process broke, and I go back to fix that before pushing."

### "Tell me about a time you didn't hit your numbers"
> "Early in my first year at Ethan Allen I had a slow month — I'd put time into
> three proposals that didn't close. What I realized was that I'd been pitching to
> couples without understanding both partners' priorities, and I was losing them at
> the 'decision' stage. I changed my process to always consult both partners
> separately before presenting, and my close rate recovered the next month."

## Admin/coordinator questions

### "Tell me about your Microsoft Office skills"
> "I use Excel daily — pivot tables, conditional formatting, basic formulas. I
> built my client pipeline tracker at Ethan Allen in Excel. I use Outlook for
> calendar and email management, Word for proposals and documentation, PowerPoint
> for client presentations. I'm comfortable learning new tools quickly — I
> typically pick up new software within a week."

### "How do you prioritize when everything feels urgent?"
> "I ask two questions: who's affected if this slips, and what's the next action?
> If a dropped detail blocks someone else, that moves to the top. If it's something
> I can triage by batching, I batch. I also check in with my manager weekly on what
> she considers most important — her priorities aren't always obvious to me by
> default."

## Questions YOU should ask them (always ask 2-3)

Top picks — these signal you're serious:

1. **"What does success look like in this role after 6 months?"**
   (Shows outcome focus)

2. **"What's the biggest challenge you're hoping this hire will solve?"**
   (Shows you want to contribute to their problem)

3. **"How do you describe the culture of this team day-to-day?"**
   (Open-ended, forces them to be specific)

4. **"What's the career trajectory for someone in this role — where do strong performers typically go next?"**
   (Signals ambition without overcommitting)

5. **"What do you enjoy most about working here?"**
   (Makes them sell the company back to you — and you learn a lot from how they answer)

**NEVER ask:** salary, benefits, time off, remote policy in the FIRST interview.
Those come in screening call or offer negotiation.

## Closing the interview

> "Thank you for the time. Based on what you've shared, I'm even more interested
> than when I walked in. My biggest takeaway is [specific thing they said]. What
> are the next steps, and when should I expect to hear from you?"

Ask for the next step. Interviewers remember candidates who ask for it.
