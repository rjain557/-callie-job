# STAR Stories — Interview Prep

STAR = Situation → Task → Action → Result
Use these as the backbone of every behavioral interview answer. Memorize the numbers,
keep the stories natural, and adapt the framing to each job.

## Story Bank

### Story 1 — The $800K Achievement (PRIMARY HERO STORY)

**Situation:** Three years ago I joined Ethan Allen as a Design Consultant during a
period when residential design sales were under pressure from online furniture retailers.

**Task:** My role was to generate design sales through in-home consultations and
showroom engagements with clients who could go to countless other options instead.

**Action:** Rather than leading with product, I led with the client. I invested
heavily in the first 30 minutes of every consultation understanding what they
actually wanted their space to feel like. I built a system for tracking every
client's preferences, past projects, and timing so follow-ups felt personal not
transactional. I developed strong vendor relationships so I could promise realistic
timelines and keep my word.

**Result:** Over three years I generated over $800,000 in design sales and earned the
2025 Gold Spirit Award — an award given to the top performing consultants across the
company. More importantly, I built a repeat-client practice where clients came back
for second and third rooms, and referred friends and family. That was the long-term
metric that mattered most to me.

**Reflection:** What I learned is that in luxury design sales, trust is the entire
product. The furniture is the outcome, but the trust is what earns the sale and the
referral.

### Story 2 — Building a Difficult Client Relationship

**Situation:** One client at Ethan Allen came in with strong opinions that conflicted
with their husband's taste, and every design decision became a negotiation between
them.

**Task:** I needed to find a way to help them co-create a space that felt like their
home together, not a compromise neither of them loved.

**Action:** Instead of pushing through product selections, I paused the process for a
full consultation dedicated to understanding each of their priorities separately. I
asked them to list the five elements of a space that matter most to each of them,
independently. Then I showed them where their lists overlapped and where they differed.
When we resumed design work, every choice was framed as "this serves both your priority
of X and his priority of Y."

**Result:** They completed a $40,000+ living and dining room project, both signed off
happily, and they referred two neighbors over the next year — one of whom became
another major client.

**Reflection:** People don't disagree because they want different things. They
disagree because they haven't articulated what they actually want. My job was to help
them articulate it, then make the decisions inevitable.

### Story 3 — Coordination Under Pressure

**Situation:** At Vintage Design Inc., we had a peak period when installers, vendors,
and clients were all converging on the same few days, and our front desk — which I
managed — was the single point of coordination.

**Task:** I needed to keep everyone on schedule while maintaining accurate
documentation and not letting any detail slip.

**Action:** I built a shared daily schedule in a shared Excel workbook that installers
could see from their phones. I created standardized client intake forms so nothing
about a consultation was left to memory. When conflicts arose between vendor delivery
times and client availability, I triaged by what was most time-sensitive and
communicated reschedules within the same business hour.

**Result:** We delivered every project on time that quarter with zero client
complaints about communication. The owner mentioned in my review that our daily
operational cadence had visibly improved.

**Reflection:** Operations work isn't about being more organized — it's about
building systems that let the organization be lighter. A good system does the
remembering for you.

---

### Story 4 — Learning Something New Quickly

**Situation:** When I started at Ethan Allen, I hadn't worked in a showroom
environment at that scale before. Previous roles had been at boutique firms.

**Task:** I needed to ramp up on Ethan Allen's product catalog, systems, and
procedures quickly without becoming the newbie slowing my team down.

**Action:** I spent my first two weekends going through the online product catalog
end-to-end so I could speak fluently about any collection by day two. I shadowed the
top-performing consultant for three half-days to learn the consultation flow she
used. I kept a running notes doc of every question I had, researched answers on my
own time, and brought prepared follow-up questions to my manager rather than
interrupting her workflow.

**Result:** By my fourth week, my close rate matched the team average. By month six,
I was in the top quartile. By year one, I was top 10%.

**Reflection:** Speed of ramp isn't about IQ — it's about how many of your questions
you answer yourself before asking someone else.

### Story 5 — Disagreeing with a Vendor

**Situation:** At Goff Designs, I was processing a vendor work order that I realized
had the wrong fabric SKU — the one on the order would have clashed badly with the
client's existing furniture.

**Task:** The vendor rep on the phone was confident the SKU was correct because that's
what was on the purchase order. Our principal designer wasn't available. I had to
decide whether to push back or let it go.

**Action:** I politely asked the rep to pull up the original email thread and the
design board. I walked them through the fabric we'd specified, the palette of the
room, and why the SKU they had would conflict. I then proposed the correct SKU and
asked them to update the order and resend the confirmation.

**Result:** The vendor caught their data entry error and corrected it. The client's
project went forward with the right fabric, and I earned trust with the principal
designer for protecting her design intent when she wasn't there to do it herself.

**Reflection:** Junior roles aren't about being passive. They're about knowing your
judgment well enough to push back when you see something wrong.

### Story 6 — Handling a Rejected Proposal

**Situation:** Early in my Ethan Allen career, I put three weeks into a proposal for a
bedroom and bath refresh, and the client ultimately said no and went with another firm.

**Task:** I had to figure out why — without being defensive or feeling personally
rejected.

**Action:** I asked the client for honest feedback. She told me my proposal had great
product selections but the timeline was too aggressive for her life, and she felt
pressured during the close. I thanked her and asked if I could revise the proposal
with a longer timeline. She said yes. Three weeks later, she signed for a scaled-back
version.

**Result:** A $15,000 sale that would have been zero. More importantly, I changed how
I paced every close after that — I never again tried to push a timeline that didn't
match the client's life.

**Reflection:** The best feedback is the sale you lost. Ask for it.

---

### Story 7 — Managing Without Authority

**Situation:** At Ethan Allen I wasn't a manager, but newer consultants started asking
me for advice on how I was closing consistently.

**Task:** I needed to help them without stepping on my manager's role or creating
dependency.

**Action:** I set up informal 30-minute "office hours" every two weeks where anyone
could come with specific questions. I made it clear these were peer conversations, not
training sessions. I also documented my consultation flow so people could reference it
on their own.

**Result:** Three newer consultants credited those sessions with improving their close
rates. My manager thanked me for contributing to team performance. And it gave me
informal leadership experience I'd use in any future manager-track role.

**Reflection:** You don't need a title to be a leader. You need to make it easy for
people to get what they need.

---

### Story 8 — Conflict with a Colleague

**Situation:** A colleague at Ethan Allen had a habit of picking up walk-in clients
who I was actively engaged with, which cost me sales.

**Task:** I needed to address it without escalating to a manager or poisoning our
working relationship.

**Action:** I invited her for coffee outside of work and opened with the fact that I
valued our working relationship. Then I described the specific situations where I felt
my clients had been reassigned. I asked her if I had misread anything. Turned out she
had interpreted some of my client hand-offs to the design desk as me releasing them —
not as active engagement. We established a simple "tagging" system in our shared
calendar to prevent it.

**Result:** Zero conflict for the next two years. We became one of the strongest
collaborators on the team.

**Reflection:** Most workplace conflict is interpretation, not intent. Ask first,
accuse never.

## Preparation ritual before every interview

1. Re-read the job description — highlight the 3-5 key requirements
2. Map your stories to those requirements — which stories prove which skills?
3. Rehearse 2-3 stories out loud, timed at 90 seconds each
4. Pick one question you'll ask the interviewer that shows you researched the company
5. Review `company-intel/{company-name}.md` if we have it
6. Breathe. You've earned every number in your stories.
